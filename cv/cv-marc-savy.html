Skills, Experience and Ambitions

At present I am the project lead of Apiman, an open-source JVM-based API management platform. Apiman is designed to meet the requirements of large enterprise entities such as banks, stock exchanges, and trading platforms, looking to scale to millions of requests per second (Red Hats traditional customers); it combines well-tested robustness with scalability, flexibility, and loose coupling. Whilst primarily HTTP-focussed it can support other protocols and intersects with a broad range of other technologies. We have multiple implementations, including a servlet implementation uses familiar Java EE technologies, a Vert.x implementation that uses cutting-edge async technologies, and a Jetty-based microservice implementation (amongst others).

Since taking the leadership role nearly 2 years ago, I’ve been responsible for all aspects of the project: technical architecture, roadmap, design, implementation and testing; community engagement, support, and contributions; and behind-the-scenes aspects, such as working with product management, advocation, blogging, and social media. It is a broad role with a substantial degree of autonomy, requiring soft skills and a willingness to collaborate both internally and externally, in addition to technical aspects. Running an open source project necessitates balancing the needs of the community with internal business requirements and priorities, in terms of time, features, effort, etc.

I was fortunate to be involved with Apiman since its inception; being a greenfield project allowed me to influence the project and participate in key decisions from the start. Even before adopting my leadership role, I enjoyed a large amount of responsibility in research, design, and implementation of various core parts of the Apiman. Of particular note, I designed much of the asynchronous core of Apimans gateway, in addition to being the principal developer of the async Vert.x gateway implementation (a strictly non-blocking, asynchronous, and message-driven platform).

As a small team, we worked extremely closely together over an extended period of time, and I appreciate the value that close-knit and equitable collaboration gave in fuelling our rapid innovation and productivity. Given the breadth of work that we undertook, each member needed to delve into all areas of the project when required; with the willingness and ability to be extremely flexible, a desire to rapidly learn new technologies, and the requirement to take ownership of key tasks from inception to completion. I am keen to continue working in an enriching, positive, and fast-paced environment, tackling stimulating challenges in collaboration with talented people.

From a technological perspective API management is an exciting place to be; at the nexus of a myriad of fast-moving technologies, it is necessary to be agile and willing to constantly continue learning. The pace and breadth of innovation in the sector rewards flexibility and punishes recalcitrance, and I have embraced those challenges, relishing the variety it provides.

As the project is so large, it’s difficult to enumerate all of the interesting work I had the opportunity to undertake. However, major tasks included a message-driven Vert.x 3 implementation of the Apiman gateway; security focussed tasks, implementing OAuth2 and mutual authentication; building asynchronous abstraction layers to provide embeddability of the Apiman gateway into multiple platforms; working with NoSQL data-stores, such as Riak, Infinispan and ElasticSearch; and collaborating on the Angular port, maintenance and development of the Apiman Manager. I have also written numerous guides, documentation, and blogs, in addition to community engagement on our public mailing lists, GitHub, IRC and email.

I also wrote and maintain various Apiman metrics implementations including Prometheus and InfluxDB; set up performance testing and profiling with Gatling; was involved in several distributed registry implementations, on various platforms including Vert.x and Elasticsearch; and produced abstracted asynchronous implementations for services such as JDBC and LDAP. We also worked with other groups in Red Hat on novel rate limiting algorithms, security issues, and user experience.

Most recently I reengineered Apimans Elasticsearch support to upgrade to Elasticsearch 5.x which required extensive changes throughout the codebase. I also contributed a key part of Red Hat’s implementation of MicroProfile OpenApi 3 to Thorntail, which is now part of the SmallRye initiative.

I previously had the fortune of working within a larger xPaaS group that develops Fabric8, an open-source Kubernetes-based microservices integration platform that is now part of OpenShift IO. Apiman provided Fabric8’s API management functionality, and I am familiar with microservices through the course of our integration work and various collaborations. It’s clear that containerisation and microservices represent a genuine movement in the industry, and will displace traditional virtualisation for most use-cases. In many ways, microservices are another less formalised rendition of the SOA-style development methodology, especially with regards to the philosophies of decomposition and reuse. Naturally, these kind of architectures introduce their own challenges, and distributed systems skills are important for any microservices deployment.

Over the years I had the fortune to liaise with customers such as MasterCard, Capital One, BP, Deloitte and Allianz to integrate their feedback into our project; requirements such as OAuth2 and Mutual Auth were added to our roadmap and delivered into the community shortly afterwards. I view it as extremely valuable for engineers to meet their technical counterparts working for customers; it provides an unfiltered understanding of the customers' needs and helps guide the direction of the project to more accurately represent the market rather than relying on hypotheticals. We had extensive uptake internally and externally, and I gained experience in assisting customers, community members, and colleagues in integrating Apiman into their solutions. It was particularly useful to develop the expertise needed to deal with external parties, where they are not as familiar with the product as the technical team, and where you likely do not have any prior knowledge of their software. Many of these have been large entities, such as banks and oil companies, who are interested in utilising Apiman within their existing infrastructure, as well as some greenfield deployments (such as SOA-based, traditional RESTful setups, etc).

Unfortunately, Red Hat decided to acquire a competitor (3scale) for reasons of expediency; market analyst visibility being a primary driver. Despite plans to the contrary, our efforts were sidelined after 3scale’s leadership took control, but Apiman has always been highly regarded and successful in the community and amongst early-adopter customers who were extremely disappointed with Red Hat’s decision.

In my time at Red Hat, I have had exposure to a rich variety of technologies: messaging, microservices, application servers, Enterprise Java, distributed systems, amongst others. In the past I was involved with BoxGrinder, a virtual machine builder written in Ruby, which could deploy and execute its images to various clouds, including AWS; I gained useful practical experience operating in a cloud environment.

At Red Hat we work in a highly distributed workforce, and I find it completely natural to cooperate with colleagues who span multiple time-zones, cultures, and customs. I’ve been extremely fortunate to work with, and be advised by, a variety of brilliant people.

I have the pleasure of delivering a number of talks at conferences and directly to customers; providing valuable feedback and enhancing Apiman’s visibility. These have include strategic customers from around the world, such as the ADF, BP, and Coles amongst others.

I would be excited to work in a role where I am able to extend my experiences and skills beyond my current environment; I have no fear of taking on new challenges, and am capable of rapidly learning and becoming proficient with new technologies as needed. I want to make a positive impact in a project that matters, with a company driven by an ethos that is genuinely trying to make a difference to people’s lives.
